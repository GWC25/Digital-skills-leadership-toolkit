Section 1 · What I Am Proposing

Two proposals. One direction.

Both of these are consultation documents. I am not presenting them as finished thinking. I am presenting them as a starting point for a conversation I want to have about how we approach digital skills development and digital inclusion across the college.

The work I have done in the quality team over the past two months has made one thing very clear to me. Digital skills development is already happening across the college in most areas. The problem is that it is not consistently named, not consistently evidenced, and not consistently connected to the frameworks that inspectors, funders, and employers are looking for. These two proposals are my attempt to address that in a structured and sustainable way.

Proposal One — The Digital Skills Framework Consultation

The Digital Skills Framework is a reference document I have developed to create a shared language for digital skills conversations across all 30 curriculum areas. It maps each area to the DfE Essential Digital Skills categories and to the industry-specific tools that employers in those sectors actually use. It is not a policy document. It is a coaching tool — designed to be opened in every Head of Area conversation as a starting point rather than a conclusion.

What I have found so far is that most areas are doing more digital than they realise. The challenge is that it is not being framed as digital skills development, so it does not get evidenced as such. The framework gives us a shared vocabulary for surfacing what is already there and connecting it to something inspectors, employers, and Skills England will recognise.

What the DSF proposes to do

Create a consistent reference point for coaching conversations with Heads of Area. Give TLAMs and the quality team a shared language for planning digital hours in Business Plans. Connect what learners are already doing in vocational practice to the EDS categories that the renewed Ofsted EIF and Skills England 2026 mandate are looking for.

What the DSF does not do

It does not mandate tools or delivery approaches. It does not assume that any digital skills development is already happening in any particular area. It is a conversation starter. The quality of the mapping will be improved by the HoA conversations that follow, and it will need updating as that work progresses.

Proposal Two — Learning Without Barriers Consultation

Learning Without Barriers is my proposal for how we approach digital inclusion across the college. It is built on three things I feel strongly about: Universal Design for Learning, Quality First Teaching, and the principle that accessible and inclusive digital practice is not something we add for certain learners — it is something we build in from the start for everyone.

If we design our digital learning environments so that they work for our most excluded learners — ESOL learners, SEND learners, learners without reliable devices or connectivity — they will work better for all learners. WCAG 2.2 AA accessibility, consistent Teams environments, and staff who understand the assistive technology available to them are not specialist provision. They are the foundation of good teaching in a digital age.

The alignment I am trying to make

Quality First Teaching tells us that every learner should access learning through high-quality, inclusive, differentiated teaching without needing separate intervention. UDL gives us the research framework for how to design that. A digital-first approach to teaching, learning and assessment is the practical environment where both can be realised. If our Teams environments are well structured, our resources are accessible by design, and our staff are confident with the tools available to them, we create the conditions where QFT and UDL principles are met as a matter of course rather than as an afterthought.

How these two proposals connect

The DSF tells us what digital skills we should be developing and where. Learning Without Barriers tells us how we create the conditions for every learner to access that development equitably. They are two sides of the same question: are we developing digital skills, and can every learner participate fully in that development? The coaching plan that follows is how we make progress on both.

The external case for both

Skills England 2026 positions digital literacy alongside English and Maths as a national priority. The renewed Ofsted EIF from November 2025 grades Inclusion as a standalone area for the first time. The WEF Future of Jobs Report 2025 identifies AI literacy, Big Data, and Digital skills as the three fastest-growing global competencies for 2025 to 2030. The West of England LSIP names Aerospace, Advanced Engineering, and Health and Social Care as founding priority sectors — all with entry-level digital fluency requirements. The case is made externally. What we need is an internal structure that rises to meet it.

Framing Digital Skills for Vocational and Industry Contexts

How digital skills are framed makes an enormous difference to whether staff and learners engage with them. What I have found is that the most effective framing always connects the digital skill directly to an industry outcome the learner already cares about. The technology becomes invisible — it is just how you do the job.

The principle I keep coming back to

Never teach the technology. Teach the task that technology enables. A construction learner does not need to learn about QR codes. They need to clock in on site, submit a digital site diary, and order materials from a supplier portal. The apps are how they do it. When you frame it that way — across every curriculum area — digital stops being a separate burden and becomes part of the fabric of vocational practice.

The 21CLD connection

The 21st Century Learning Design framework describes knowledge building, real-world problem-solving, skilled communication, and self-regulation as the learning activities most likely to develop the skills learners need for work and life. All of these are most richly realised when digital tools are embedded in authentic vocational tasks rather than taught in isolation. The authentic context framing approach is where these principles are already alive in good vocational teaching — you do not need to name the framework to work within it.

SEND, Inclusion and Accessibility

This section underpins the Learning Without Barriers proposal. The principle here is one I feel strongly about: accessibility is not a bolt-on for certain learners. The best digital accessibility practice benefits everyone. Captions help in noisy environments. Clear navigation reduces anxiety. Accessible colour contrast helps in poor lighting. Design it in from the start and you serve every learner better.

The WCAG 2.2 AA standard

WCAG 2.2 AA is the internationally recognised standard for accessible digital content and the legally required standard for UK public sector organisations. It should be the minimum standard for every digital resource created by any member of staff. The key requirements: minimum 4.5:1 colour contrast ratio for normal text, alternative text descriptions on all images, correct heading structure in all documents, captions on all video content, and keyboard navigability. The Microsoft Office Accessibility Checker checks most of these automatically — it is built into every Office application and takes seconds to run.

Section 3 · Learning Without Barriers

A digital inclusion proposal built on solid foundations

Learning Without Barriers is my proposal for how we approach digital inclusion across the college. It is built on three interconnected pillars and grounded in UDL principles, Quality First Teaching, and WCAG 2.2 AA accessibility standards. This is a consultation document — for discussion and challenge.

Learning Without Barriers — full Word documentThe complete digital inclusion strategy: five principles, three pillars, free tool guidance, WCAG 2.2 AA requirements, and specific provision for FIP, ESOL, and SEND learners.
↓ Download LWB

The Post-16 Education and Skills White Paper from November 2025 makes digital assumptions about learners, staff, and infrastructure that do not reflect the reality for a significant proportion of our cohort. Learners in coastal and rural areas around Weston-super-Mare face connectivity gaps. Many ESOL and SEND learners face device poverty. Staff confidence with digital tools varies considerably across the college. Learning Without Barriers is my response to that gap between assumption and reality.

The vision I am working towards

Every learner and member of staff at Weston College can access, engage with, and benefit from digital learning — regardless of their ability, language, background, connectivity, or prior experience. That is the standard I am proposing we set. It is ambitious. It is also achievable, because most of the tools we need to get there are already available to us.

The Five Strategic Principles

PrincipleWhat it means in practice
1. Inclusion by design, not by reactionDigital inclusion must be built into every process, system, and resource from the start — not retrofitted after the fact. This applies to curriculum design, staff development, IT procurement, and learner communications. WCAG 2.2 AA is the floor, not the ceiling.
2. Start with our most excluded learnersESOL and SEND learners face the sharpest digital barriers. If we design our systems and resources to work for them, they will work better for everyone. These communities are the proof of concept and the quality standard.
3. Everyone is responsibleDigital inclusion is not the responsibility of one team or one role. It is owned by the whole college — teaching staff, support teams, IT, student services, and leadership. The Learning Without Barriers proposal will not work if it lives only in the digital pedagogy team.
4. Quick wins build momentumTransformation takes time. The quickest and most impactful actions use free tools already available — primarily Microsoft 365 and Jisc resources. Immersive Reader, Live Captions, the Accessibility Checker, and Microsoft Translator cost nothing and are available right now.
5. Learner voice is non-negotiableLearners — particularly ESOL, SEND, and young people from disadvantaged backgrounds — must help shape this strategy as genuine co-designers, not as a consultation tick-box exercise. What they tell us about their experience of digital provision should change what we do.

The Three Pillars

Pillar 1 — Foundations

Accessibility as standard practice across all digital resources. WCAG 2.2 AA compliance for all uploaded materials. Immersive Reader, Live Captions, and the Office Accessibility Checker embedded in everyday staff practice. A consistent Teams environment structure college-wide — naming conventions, channel structure, accessible file formats. The Jisc FE Accessibility Training Matrix as the backbone of staff development in this area.

Pillar 2 — Systems and Culture

Consistent Teams environment standards across all curriculum areas. Separate CPD pathways for staff digital skills and learner digital skills — they are not the same need. Staff Foundation Awareness of AI (Jisc Module 1) before any AI tool is introduced into teaching. A Digital Resource Quality Standard — a simple checklist that every resource passes before it is uploaded. The Jisc Building Digital Capability tool as the staff baseline.

Pillar 3 — Equity and Empowerment

Genuine learner voice in shaping digital provision. Partnership with Good Things Foundation for device and data support via the National Databank — free SIM cards and data for learners without connectivity. Weston College as a community digital inclusion asset, not just an internal provider. A Student Digital Inclusion Advisory Group that has real influence and a genuine feedback loop.

The UDL alignment I am making

Universal Design for Learning asks us to provide multiple means of representation, multiple means of action and expression, and multiple means of engagement for all learners. Every tool in the table above addresses at least one of those three principles — and most address more than one. Immersive Reader provides multiple means of representation. Speech-to-text provides multiple means of expression. Real-world digital tasks provide multiple means of engagement. This is not a separate UDL programme. It is UDL already, if we choose to name it and build it in consistently.

The Connection to Quality First Teaching

QFT tells us that every learner should access learning through high-quality, inclusive, differentiated teaching without needing a separate intervention. The tools above are not interventions. They are the standard environment that makes QFT achievable in a digital-first teaching context. When a staff member runs the Accessibility Checker before uploading a resource, they are doing QFT. When they enable Live Captions as a default in every Teams session, they are doing QFT. When they show Immersive Reader to every learner at induction rather than only those with a recorded need, they are doing QFT. The Learning Without Barriers proposal is, at its heart, a practical QFT framework for the digital age.

IT Competence vs Digital Fluency — The Distinction That Matters Most in HoA Conversations

The most common starting point in coaching conversations is a Head of Area saying something like "we're not really digital" or "our learners already know how to use technology." Both statements usually reflect the same misunderstanding. Being able to log into Teams is not a digital skill. Being able to use Teams professionally, collaboratively, and accessibly — that is.

The principle I return to every time

Never teach the technology. Teach the task that technology enables. A construction learner does not need to learn about QR codes. They need to clock in on site, submit a digital site diary, and order materials from a supplier portal. When it is framed that way, the digital skill is no longer a separate lesson. It is just part of doing the job.

The Digital Natives Myth — Why We Cannot Assume Competency at Enrolment

The assumption that young people are digitally competent because they are digitally connected is one of the most consequential misconceptions in Further Education. What I have found in practice is that learners are often expert in recreational digital spaces and largely lost in professional ones. The gap is not always visible at enrolment — it tends to show up when they are asked to do something functional with technology for a vocational purpose.

The mobile phone context

The DfE 2024 guidance encouraging secondary schools to restrict mobile phones has had broadly positive wellbeing outcomes. But without planned replacement through structured desktop and laptop digital skills development, many learners arriving at Weston College in 2025–26 have spent their final school years in mobile-reduced environments — and without the professional digital tool competencies that employers expect from day one. That is a gap we need to plan for, not assume away.

Glossary — Key Terms and Acronyms

Every acronym and technical term used in this document, explained in plain English.

AAC

Augmentative and Alternative Communication

Technology supporting learners who cannot rely on spoken language. Examples: Proloquo2Go, Grid 3, Tobii eye-gaze systems. Central to FIP Autism and Complex Needs provision.

ADAS

Advanced Driver Assistance Systems

Electronic safety systems fitted to modern vehicles (lane-keeping, emergency braking, parking sensors). Require specialist digital calibration after any vehicle repair. Now a mandatory EMV competency.

AI

Artificial Intelligence

Computer systems performing tasks that typically require human intelligence. AI Literacy means knowing how to use these tools critically and ethically — not just that they exist.

ASF

Adult Skills Fund

The primary DfE funding stream for adult skills provision, replacing the Adult Education Budget from August 2024. Skills Bootcamps are ASF-funded. Being devolved to Mayoral Combined Authorities from 2025.

BDC

Building Digital Capability (Jisc)

Jisc framework and self-assessment tool for measuring digital capabilities in learners and staff. The Discovery Tool is the learner-facing component. Free for Jisc subscribers. digitalcapability.jisc.ac.uk

BIM

Building Information Modelling

Digital process for creating and managing construction project information. BIM Level 2 is a legal requirement for all UK government-funded construction projects. Software: Autodesk Revit, AutoCAD.

CPD

Continuing Professional Development

Structured activities maintaining and advancing professional knowledge and skills. In this document, refers specifically to staff digital skills development: Jisc AI Literacy modules, ETF Enhance pathways, and Microsoft Learn for Educators courses.

DEI Survey

Digital Experience Insights Survey (Jisc)

Sector-benchmarked survey of learner and staff digital experience. Free for Jisc subscribers. Evidence for Ofsted Inclusion and Achievement grades. jisc.ac.uk/digital-experience-insights

DigComp 3.0

European Digital Competence Framework

European Commission framework for digital competence, updated November 2025. Five levels from Foundation to Highly Advanced. Used in DSF Tab 7. EC DigComp

DSF

Digital Skills Framework

The Weston College Digital Skills Framework — produced by the Digital Pedagogy Coach as a consultation document. Maps all 30 curriculum areas to EDS categories and industry-specific tools. A coaching tool, not a policy document.

DTPF

Digital Teaching Professional Framework (ETF)

Education and Training Foundation framework for teacher digital competence in FE. Four stages: Exploring, Adopting (Developing), Adopting (Embedding), Leading. ETF Enhance Platform

EDS

Essential Digital Skills Framework (DfE)

DfE framework defining minimum digital skills for employment and life. Five categories: Communicating; Handling Information; Transacting; Problem Solving; Being Safe and Legal Online. gov.uk EDS

EIF

Education Inspection Framework (Ofsted)

Ofsted's inspection framework, renewed November 2025. Replaced single-word judgements with a five-point Report Card across six evaluation areas. Safeguarding judged separately as Met or Not Met. GOV.UK EIF

EPA

End Point Assessment

The final assessment of an apprenticeship standard, conducted by an independent assessor. All EPA standards require digital evidence — ePortfolios, digital CPD logs, and Smart Assessor records are the primary mechanisms.

ESOL

English for Speakers of Other Languages

English language learning for adults whose first language is not English. ESOL learners face compound barriers: language and digital exclusion simultaneously. 118,220 learners nationally (DfE 2025).

EV

Electric Vehicle

A vehicle powered entirely or partly by an electric motor. EV diagnostics are entirely digital — battery management and ADAS calibration require new digital competencies that are now non-negotiable for all modern vehicle technicians.

GDPR

General Data Protection Regulation

UK and EU legal framework governing collection, storage, and use of personal data. All staff and learners handling personal data must understand their GDPR obligations.

HoA

Head of Area

The curriculum leader responsible for a specific subject area at Weston College. The primary audience for digital pedagogy coaching conversations and the gateway to embedded digital skills development for learners in their area.

LSIP

Local Skills Improvement Plan

DfE-funded, employer-led research identifying local skills gaps and post-16 training priorities. The West of England and North Somerset LSIP is led by Business West. businesswest.co.uk LSIP

MTD

Making Tax Digital (HMRC)

HMRC programme requiring digital VAT submission and tax record-keeping. Mandatory for all self-employed practitioners from 2026. A direct employability digital skill for HBH learners who will trade as self-employed practitioners.

OBD II

On-Board Diagnostics Version 2

Standardised digital diagnostic port fitted to all vehicles from 1996. OBD II scanners read fault codes, live data, and sensor outputs. Modern vehicles cannot be diagnosed without one — a non-negotiable digital skill for all EMV learners.

QFT

Quality First Teaching

A whole-class teaching approach ensuring all learners access high-quality, inclusive, differentiated teaching without requiring separate intervention. The Learning Without Barriers proposal aligns digital practice to QFT principles throughout.

SEND

Special Educational Needs and Disabilities

Learners who have additional learning, physical, sensory, or social and emotional needs. Weston is a former SEND Centre of Excellence. Digital inclusion for SEND learners is a direct obligation under the renewed Ofsted Inclusion grade.

SLT

Senior Leadership Team

The college's senior management team responsible for strategic direction, resource allocation, and quality oversight.

TLAM

Teaching, Learning and Assessment Manager

The role responsible for quality of teaching, learning, and assessment within a faculty or group of curriculum areas. Key partner in embedding digital skills development into area quality cycles.

UDL

Universal Design for Learning

A research-backed framework for designing learning that works for all learners from the outset. Three principles: multiple means of representation, action and expression, and engagement. udlguidelines.cast.org

WCAG 2.2 AA

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines

International standard for accessible digital content. Level AA is legally required for UK public sector organisations. Key requirements: 4.5:1 colour contrast, alt-text on images, correct heading structure, captions on video. WCAG 2.2 Quick Reference

WEF

World Economic Forum

International organisation publishing the biennial Future of Jobs Report. The 2025 edition identifies AI Orchestration, Big Data, and Digital Literacy as the top three fastest-growing skills globally for 2025–2030. WEF Future of Jobs 2025

21CLD

21st Century Learning Design

A research framework identifying learning activities most likely to develop the skills learners need for work and life: knowledge building, real-world problem-solving, skilled communication, collaboration, use of ICT for learning, and self-regulation.
