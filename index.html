Section 1 · The Framework

The Digital Learning Pyramid

Three levels. One direction of travel. Everything in this toolkit — every skill, every tool, every CPD lever, every coaching conversation — is organised in relation to these three levels. The Pyramid is built bottom-up: you cannot build inclusion without foundations, and you cannot innovate sustainably without inclusion.

Why a Pyramid?

A pyramid communicates sequence and dependency. Every member of staff is somewhere on this journey. This toolkit is designed to help them know where they are, what comes next, and how to get there — with support that is proportionate to where they are starting from.

The Three Levels

Level 1Digital Foundations — including Digital Accessibility

Every member of staff can use the tools available to them with confidence. Devices, software, email, digital learning environments, and cloud storage are used reliably and professionally. Accessibility is a professional standard from day one, not an add-on. Every digital resource passes the Office Accessibility Checker before it is shared. WCAG 2.2 AA is the floor. Immersive Reader, Live Captions, and the Accessibility Checker are part of everyday practice.

Level 2Digital Inclusion — where true Digital TLA Pedagogy lives

Staff use digital tools purposefully to improve teaching, learning, and assessment for all learners. They understand how to use assistive technology, design inclusive digital environments, and adapt their practice for ESOL, SEND, and disadvantaged learners. Teams environments are well structured, Assignments use rubrics, and learners receive accessible digital feedback. This level aligns with UDL and Quality First Teaching principles. Staff here have moved beyond personal competence into pedagogical application.

Level 3Digital Innovation — Leading Change and Shaping Practice

Staff at this level are not just using digital tools. They are redesigning what teaching looks like with them. They innovate with AI, build automated workflows, challenge assumptions about what is possible, and influence others. They are Digital Leads, Digital Champions, and agents of change. Their practice does not stay in their classroom. It spreads.

♾

Learning Without Barriers — the thread through all three levels

At Level 1: every resource is accessible and every learner knows about Immersive Reader. At Level 2: AT is offered proactively to all, not just those with a recorded need. At Level 3: staff advocate for AT investment and champion WCAG 2.2 AA as a non-negotiable institutional standard. The ambition is the same at every level — just the depth of action changes.

What is the ETF DTPF?

ETF DTPF — Digital Teaching Professional Framework — explained in plain English

Who made it: The Education and Training Foundation (ETF) — the national body for the further education and skills sector in England.

What it is: A framework that describes what good digital teaching looks like in FE, in four progressive stages from Exploring to Leading. It is the closest thing FE has to a nationally recognised standard for teacher digital skills.

Why it matters here: The four ETF DTPF stages map directly to the four stages in this toolkit (Stage 1 = Foundations; Stages 2A/2B = Inclusion; Stage 3 = Innovation). When staff complete ETF modules, they are building evidenceable CPD against a nationally recognised framework — not just completing training.

How to access it: Free for all FE sector staff via etfoundation.co.uk/professional-development (MyETF platform from April 2026). The DTPF self-assessment is the starting point for any coaching conversation about teaching digital skills.

What is DigCompEdu — and do we need it?

DigCompEdu — European Educator Digital Competence Framework — explained

Who made it: The European Commission Joint Research Centre — the EU's science and knowledge service.

What it is: A six-level progression framework (A1 through C2) describing the digital competence of educators across six areas: professional engagement; digital resources; teaching and learning; assessment; learner empowerment; and facilitating digital competence.

Why it appears in this toolkit: It provides a shared European language for educator digital skills that goes beyond any single national framework. The six-level scale (A1 Newcomer → C2 Pioneer) is used here to show where each ETF DTPF stage sits on a wider progression map.

How closely should we follow it? We use DigCompEdu as a reference lens, not a compliance requirement. The ETF DTPF is our primary framework for FE. DigCompEdu is useful for: positioning staff development conversations; evidencing quality at inspection; connecting to European digital education agendas. You do not need to use the DigCompEdu jargon with staff — the Pyramid stages are sufficient for day-to-day conversations.

Self-assessment tool: joint-research-centre.ec.europa.eu/digcompedu_en — free, self-paced, generates a personal profile.

The Learn, Work, Life alignment

Why Learn, Work, Life matters — not just for learners but for the whole framework

The college's three-context model connects digital skills to what they are actually for. Learn: how staff and learners develop digital capability for study and professional growth. Work: how digital skills translate into employment-ready practice in vocational and professional contexts. Life: how inclusive digital practice enables every learner to participate fully in society, access services, and live independently. Every level of the Pyramid speaks to all three contexts. Every coaching conversation should connect the skill being developed to at least one of them.

Teacher Digital Skills are divided into two lists at each stage: what staff MUST develop to meet the professional standard at that level, and what they MIGHT explore as they move towards the next stage. This distinction directly addresses the question: "to do this, you need xyz."

How to use MUST and MIGHT

MUST skills are the non-negotiable baseline for every teacher at that stage. They are the things a TLAM should be able to see evidence of during a Learning Walk or Developmental Observation. MIGHT skills are aspirational — staff moving towards the next stage, or those with particular interest areas, can explore these. They are not expected of everyone at this level.

Section 3 · The Teacher Framework

Four stages. One journey.

A fully expanded framework for FE teaching staff at Weston College. Each stage sets out the skills staff should be demonstrating, the tools available to them, and how development is supported. Particularly designed to support new teachers with a clear "to do this, you need xyz" pathway.

New to Weston College? Start here.

If you are a new teacher, your journey starts at Stage 1 (Exploring). The pathway below shows what the first term looks like, step by step. You are not expected to be at Stage 3 on day one — or even in year one. You are expected to build strong foundations and establish accessibility as a professional habit from the outset.

New Teacher Pathway — Your First Term

Week 1–2
Induction (NSI)
Digital onboarding — your starting foundation

Log into Teams, Outlook, OneDrive, and SharePoint. Complete the NSI digital onboarding tasks. Set up your class Team using the Education template. Run the Accessibility Checker on your first resource before uploading. Attend the Digital TLA session in the Extended NSI programme.

Weeks 3–6
Half-term 1
Build your class environment

Complete the Jisc Discovery Tool Teaching Staff question set — this gives you a personal digital skills profile and generates recommended resources. Create your first Teams Assignment with a rubric attached. Demonstrate Immersive Reader to your class at induction. Start the ETF DTPF Exploring stage modules (free, self-paced, 4 hours total).

Half-term 2
Moving on
Formative assessment and inclusion

Create your first Microsoft Forms quiz embedded in Teams. Begin exploring inclusive tools proactively — not just for learners with recorded needs. Have your first 1:1 coaching conversation with the Digital Pedagogy Coach to review your Jisc profile and set your next development targets.

Term 2+
Developing
Moving from Stage 1 to Stage 2A

Complete Jisc AI Literacy Module 1 — the prerequisite for any Copilot or AI tool use in teaching. Attend a half-termly Teach Meet and share one digital practice example. Review your Jisc Discovery Tool profile with your TLAM. Begin the ETF DTPF Adopting stage modules.

Two dimensions at every stage

This framework covers two inseparable dimensions at every stage. General Digital Skills: the personal competencies every staff member needs to work effectively in a digital organisation. Teaching Digital Skills: the pedagogical capabilities that translate personal competence into high quality, inclusive teaching. Both dimensions are present at every stage. The difference is in how they are applied and evidenced.

This is a development tool — not an audit tool

When a TLAM or Digital Pedagogy Coach sits down with a member of staff, the question is not "which boxes can you tick?" The question is: "where are you now, what does the next stage look like for you, and what support is available to help you get there?" The Jisc Discovery Tool provides the baseline. This framework provides the roadmap.

Section 4 · CPD and Development

How staff grow — through the Quality cycle

The Professional Development Strategy in the Quality Handbook 2025/26 provides the mechanisms through which digital skills development is supported. The key principle — digital CPD is embedded, not added

Digital skills development does not require a separate programme. The mechanisms are already in the Quality Handbook. The Digital Pedagogy Coach role connects the existing CPD infrastructure to the digital skills framework — so that every Learning Walk, every Teach Meet, every CQRP conversation becomes an opportunity to surface and strengthen digital practice.

Section 5 · Learning Without Barriers

Inclusion is not an add-on. It is the design.

Learning Without Barriers is the metric that runs through every level of this framework — from Immersive Reader at induction to AT advocacy at Stage 3. The best digital accessibility practice benefits everyone: captions help in noisy environments, clear navigation reduces anxiety, accessible colour contrast helps in poor lighting. Design it in from the start and you serve every learner better.

♾

The Learning Without Barriers principle

Accessibility is not a bolt-on for certain learners. It is the professional standard for every digital resource, every digital environment, and every digital interaction. WCAG 2.2 AA is the floor — not the ceiling. Every teacher, at every stage, is responsible for this. The only thing that changes as you move up the Pyramid is the depth of your contribution: from using tools, to teaching with them, to advocating for them institutionally.

WCAG 2.2 AA — the standard we work to

What WCAG 2.2 AA requires — in plain English

WCAG 2.2 AA (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) is the internationally recognised standard for accessible digital content and the legally required standard for UK public sector organisations. Key requirements: minimum 4.5:1 colour contrast ratio for normal text; alternative text descriptions on all images; correct heading structure in all documents (H1, H2, H3 — not manual bold); captions on all video content; and keyboard navigability. The Microsoft Office Accessibility Checker checks most of these automatically — it is built into every Office application and takes seconds to run. Run it before sharing anything.

What the Accessibility Checker does automatically

✓
Missing alt textFlags any image without an alt text description. Two sentences maximum — descriptive, not decorative.
✓
Heading structureChecks that headings use built-in styles (Heading 1, 2, 3) not just bold text.
✓
Colour contrastFlags text that does not meet the 4.5:1 minimum contrast ratio.
✓
Table structureChecks that tables have header rows and are not used for layout purposes.
✓
LinksFlags non-descriptive link text (e.g. "click here") — all links should describe their destination.

What you must add manually

!
Captions on videoAuto-captions in Teams and Stream must be reviewed and corrected — they are 80–90% accurate and need checking for specialist terminology.
!
Reading orderIn complex layouts, check that a screen reader will read content in a logical order.
!
Meaningful file names"Week 3 — Photosynthesis Notes" not "Document1." Learners using screen readers navigate by file name.
!
Plain languageShort sentences, active voice, no unexplained jargon. Benefits every learner, not just those with literacy needs.

The EEF evidence — why this matters for outcomes

The Education Endowment Foundation found that when used effectively, digital technology can accelerate learning by two to three months. The key qualifier is when used effectively. Technical provision alone does not deliver the attainment gains. Pedagogical coaching — showing staff how to use these tools purposefully for learning — is what bridges the gap between available and effective.

Section 6 · Frameworks and Glossary

Making sense of the frameworks

Plain English explainers for every framework, acronym, and external standard referenced in this toolkit. Designed to answer the question: "what does this actually mean, and does it matter to me?"

The frameworks — what they are and why they matter here

ETF
DTPF
Digital Teaching Professional FrameworkEducation and Training Foundation · FE sector · England

What it is: The national framework describing what good digital teaching looks like in FE, in four progressive stages — Exploring, Adopting (Developing), Adopting (Embedding), and Leading. It is the closest thing FE has to a nationally recognised standard for teacher digital skills.

Why it matters here: The four ETF DTPF stages map directly to the four stages of this toolkit. When staff complete ETF modules, they build CPD evidence against a nationally recognised framework — not just training hours.

Who uses it: All FE teaching staff in England. Free via ETF. Used by Ofsted inspectors to frame expectations around teacher digital skills.

How closely should we follow it? The DTPF is our primary framework for teacher digital skills at Weston College. The stages in this toolkit use DTPF language. Staff should know what stage they are at and what the next stage requires.

→ etfoundation.co.uk/professional-development
Dig
Comp
Edu
European Educator Digital Competence FrameworkEuropean Commission Joint Research Centre · International

What it is: A six-level progression framework (A1 through C2) describing the digital competence of educators across six areas: professional engagement; digital resources; teaching and learning; assessment; learner empowerment; and facilitating digital competence.

Why it appears here: It provides a shared European language for educator digital skills that goes beyond any single national framework. The A1–C2 scale is used in this toolkit to show where each DTPF stage sits on a wider progression map.

How closely should we follow it? DigCompEdu is used here as a reference lens, not a compliance requirement. You do not need to use DigCompEdu jargon with staff. The Pyramid stages and DTPF stages are sufficient for day-to-day conversations. DigCompEdu is useful at Stage 2B and Stage 3 for staff who want to map their practice to an international standard and for evidence in quality and inspection contexts.

→ joint-research-centre.ec.europa.eu/digcompedu_en
Jisc
BDC
Building Digital Capability Discovery ToolJisc · FE and HE sector · Free for subscribing institutions

What it is: A self-assessment tool that produces a personal digital capability profile across six dimensions: information, data, and media literacy; digital creation, problem-solving, and innovation; communication, collaboration, and participation; digital learning and development; ICT proficiency; and digital identity and wellbeing.

Why it matters here: The Discovery Tool is the primary baseline assessment for both staff and learners at Weston College. Role-specific question sets for FE teaching staff, general staff, new students, current students, and employability skills.

How to use it: Staff complete the Teaching Staff question set at the start of the academic year. The personalised report is the starting point for every coaching conversation. Weston College subscribes to Jisc as a member — contact the Digital Pedagogy Coach for access details.

→ jisc.potential.ly/signin (direct access)
EDS
Essential Digital Skills FrameworkDepartment for Education · England · Statutory

What it is: The DfE's statutory framework defining the digital skills that adults need for work and life in England. Five categories: Using Devices and Handling Information; Creating and Editing; Communicating; Transacting; Being Safe and Responsible Online. Two qualification levels: Entry Level 3 and Level 1.

Why it matters here: Digital hours in Business Plans must be mapped to EDS categories. The EDS is what inspectors and the Skills England mandate look for when they ask about digital skills development. It is the vocabulary we use to make visible what is already happening in vocational programmes.

Who it is for: Used to plan and evidence digital skills development for learners. Staff digital skills development is captured through the ETF DTPF and Jisc BDC frameworks.

→ gov.uk — Essential Digital Skills Framework
UDL
Universal Design for LearningCAST · International research framework

What it is: A research-backed framework for designing learning that works for all learners from the outset. Three principles: multiple means of representation (how you present information), multiple means of action and expression (how learners demonstrate understanding), and multiple means of engagement (what motivates and sustains learners).

Why it matters here: UDL underpins the Digital Inclusion level of the Pyramid. When staff design accessible digital environments, use inclusive tools proactively, and differentiate digitally — they are applying UDL principles. The connection between UDL and Quality First Teaching is direct: both are about designing for all learners from the outset, not retrofitting support after the fact.

→ udlguidelines.cast.org
21CLD
21st Century Learning DesignMicrosoft Education · International framework

What it is: A research framework identifying the learning activities most likely to develop the skills learners need for work and life: knowledge building; real-world problem-solving; skilled communication; collaboration; use of ICT for learning; and self-regulation.

Why it matters here: 21CLD is the framework behind the Microsoft Certified Educator (MCE) certification. Stage 3 staff who aspire to MCE will engage with 21CLD formally. But the principles of 21CLD are already alive in good vocational teaching — you do not need to name the framework to work within it. Every authentic digital task in a vocational context is 21CLD in practice.

→ Microsoft Certified Educator — 21CLD preparation
WCAG
2.2 AA
Web Content Accessibility GuidelinesW3C · International standard · UK legally required

What it is: The internationally recognised standard for accessible digital content. Level AA is the legally required standard for UK public sector organisations and the professional standard for all digital resources at Weston College.

Why it matters here: WCAG 2.2 AA is the floor — not the ceiling — for every digital resource created by any member of staff. This is not an aspiration. It is the current legal baseline. The Microsoft Office Accessibility Checker makes compliance achievable in seconds. There is no excuse for sharing an inaccessible resource.

→ WCAG 2.2 Quick Reference — W3C
QFT
Quality First TeachingWeston College · Quality Handbook · Internal standard

What it is: Weston College's approach to high quality, inclusive, differentiated teaching without the need for separate intervention. Every learner receives excellent teaching as a matter of course — not because they have been identified as needing it.

Why it matters here: Digital tools, when designed in from the start, are the practical infrastructure of QFT in a digital age. Accessible resources, Live Captions as a default, Immersive Reader available to everyone — these are QFT in action. The Digital Learning Pyramid and QFT point in exactly the same direction.

Glossary — all terms used in this toolkit

AI Literacy

Artificial Intelligence Literacy

The ability to understand, critically evaluate, and use AI tools responsibly. At Weston College, Jisc AI Literacy Module 1 is the prerequisite for any AI tool adoption in teaching. Not the same as AI proficiency — it includes ethical awareness and understanding of limitations.

AT

Assistive Technology

Hardware and software designed to help people with disabilities access digital content and participate in learning. Includes Immersive Reader, Read & Write Gold, Tobii Eye Gaze, Proloquo2Go, Dragon Dictate, and Windows Speech Recognition. Should be offered proactively to all learners — not only those with a recorded SEND need.

BDC

Building Digital Capability (Jisc)

Jisc's service for developing digital skills in HE and FE. Includes the Discovery Tool (self-assessment), the Digital Elevation Tool (organisational assessment), and the Building Digital Capability Community of Practice. Weston College subscribes to this service.

CQRP

Curriculum Quality Review Panel

A quality review meeting that includes a TLA Overview covering Self, Peer, and QRA evidence. At Stage 2B, digital skills coaching records, RAG ratings, and Digital Health Check data all feed into CQRP evidence.

DigComp 3.0

European Digital Competence Framework for Citizens

The European Commission framework describing digital competence for all citizens — not just educators. November 2025 update. Eight levels (Foundation through Highly Specialised). Useful for positioning learner digital skills development on a European scale. Different from DigCompEdu, which is specifically for educators.

DTPF

Digital Teaching Professional Framework (ETF)

The ETF framework describing four stages of digital teaching competence: Exploring, Adopting (Developing), Adopting (Embedding), and Leading. The primary framework for teacher digital skills at Weston College. Free self-assessment and CPD modules via etfoundation.co.uk/professional-development.

EDS

Essential Digital Skills (DfE)

The DfE statutory framework for adult digital skills in England. Five categories at Entry Level 3 and Level 1. Digital hours in Business Plans must be mapped to EDS categories. The vocabulary inspectors and Skills England use.

ETF

Education and Training Foundation

The national body for the further education and skills sector in England. Produces the Digital Teaching Professional Framework (DTPF) and the Enhance digital CPD platform. Merging with SET (Society for Education and Training) into a single MyETF platform from April 2026.

Jisc AI Literacy Modules

Modules 1, 2, and 3 — Jisc National Centre for AI

Three live webinar sessions, each approximately one hour. Module 1: Understanding AI basics (prerequisite for any AI tool use in teaching). Module 2: Essential AI skills for teaching and learning. Module 3: Ethical and responsible use of AI. Free for Jisc member institutions. Access via jisc.ac.uk.

LWB

Learning Without Barriers

Weston College's commitment to ensuring that no learner is excluded from learning by their access to or confidence with technology. The thread running through every level of the Digital Learning Pyramid — from Immersive Reader at induction to AT advocacy and institutional inclusion leadership at Stage 3.

MSLE

Microsoft Learn for Educators

Microsoft's programme giving educators access to Official Microsoft Curriculum, ready-to-teach materials, lab environments, and Applied Skills credentials. Free for eligible educators. Relevant primarily at Stage 3 — for staff embedding AI and tech formally into curriculum. learn.microsoft.com/en-us/training/educator-center/programs/msle/

NSI / Extended NSI

New Staff Induction

The four-day academic staff induction at Weston College, which includes a dedicated Digital TLA session. The Extended NSI on Wednesday afternoons in Term 1 includes additional digital content. The starting point for all Stage 1 development.

QIP

Quality Improvement Plan

Each area's plan for improving the quality of education. Tab 2 links CPD and Teach Meet activity to TLA impact. Digital skills development actions sit naturally in this structure. The Digital Pedagogy Coach supports TLAMs in articulating digital AFIs and progress measures in QIPs.

QRA

Quality Review Activity

An Ofsted-style scrutiny of the quality of education, including the learning environment and digital resources available. At Stage 2B, staff should be able to demonstrate accessible resources, a well-structured Teams environment, and learner evidence of digital skills development.

TLAM

Teaching, Learning and Assessment Manager

The curriculum management role most directly connected to TLA quality at Weston College. TLAMs lead Learning Walks, Developmental Observations, Peer Reviews, CQRPs, and PPRs. The Digital Pedagogy Coach works alongside TLAMs to connect digital skills development to the quality cycle.

WCAG 2.2 AA

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines version 2.2, Level AA

The legal minimum standard for accessible digital content in UK public sector organisations. The professional standard for every digital resource created at Weston College. Key requirements: 4.5:1 colour contrast, alt text on all images, correct heading structure, captions on all video. Run the Microsoft Accessibility Checker on everything before sharing it.
